## Executive Summary

Peru is at an inflection point in its renewable energy transition. With 938 MW of solar and ~1,021 MW of wind operational today, the country holds 20,000+ MW in the development pipeline — the vast majority awaiting regulatory clearance and bankable offtake agreements. The physical deployment of this capacity will drive enormous procurement demand for Balance of System (BOS) and Balance of Plant (BOP) components over the next 3–7 years.

For a developer like Statkraft with an existing Latam footprint, Peru presents both a compelling opportunity and a complex procurement challenge. BOS/BOP supply chains in Peru are immature — heavily import-dependent for technology equipment and reliant on a thin local construction market that faces capacity constraints as project volumes accelerate.

**Key findings:**
- Solar PV capacity expected to reach 2.3 GW by end-2026; wind flat in the near term, growing sharply from 2027
- 95 projects (21+ GW) lack final concessions — procurement activity will accelerate rapidly as these unlock
- BOS for solar is ~80% import-dependent; domestic value is primarily in civil works and DC cabling
- BOP for wind is structurally more complex — turbines imported, foundations and roads local, substation equipment mixed
- Grid connection is the single largest procurement risk — $430M national transmission investment underway but insufficient for the scale of development planned
- PPA bankability remains the #1 project barrier; Law 32249 (Jan 2026) improves the framework but regulation is still being written

## 1. Peru Renewable Energy Market — Context

### 1.1 Current Installed Capacity (SEIN — National Grid)
| Technology | Current (2025) | Projected 2026 | Projected 2028 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Solar PV | 938 MW | 2,362 MW | 3,337 MW |
| Wind | 1,021 MW | 1,021 MW | 1,867 MW |
| Total Renewables | ~1,960 MW | 3,383 MW | 5,203 MW |
| Full Pipeline Potential | — | 17,120 MW | 24,500 MW |

*Source: OSINERGMIN, November 2025*

The 14x gap between the base-case scenario and the theoretical pipeline is the defining feature of Peru's market. 114 projects have Pre-Operability Studies approved by COES (the grid operator); only 19 have received final generation concessions from MINEM to break ground.

### 1.2 Key Development Regions

**Solar (PV):**
- **Ica** — Prime irradiance, flat terrain, proximity to transmission lines. Dominant solar region.
- **Arequipa / Moquegua** — Atacama fringe, extremely high irradiance (GHI >6.5 kWh/m²/day), water scarcity a factor
- **Lima Periphery** — Smaller projects, closer to demand centres

**Wind:**
- **Piura** — Strongest wind resource, hub of existing and planned capacity
- **Ica / Lambayeque** — Secondary wind corridors
- **Ancash, La Libertad** — Emerging coastal/highland sites

**Notable projects in pipeline:**
- Bayóvar Wind Farm, Piura — $1.056 billion investment
- Mórrope Wind Farm, Lambayeque — $353 million
- San Pedro Solar, Ica — 1,800 MW planned
- Villacuri Solar, Ica — 470 MW
- Inkia Energy — 1 GW+ solar operational 2025, 600 MW wind 2026
- Kallpa: Sunny Solar 204 MW (Moquegua), expanding to 309 MW in 2026

## 2. BOS — Solar Projects

### 2.1 Definition & Scope
Balance of System (BOS) encompasses all components of a solar project **excluding the PV panels themselves**:

| Component | Description | Local/Import |
|---|---|---|
| Inverters (string/central) | DC–AC conversion | 95% imported |
| Mounting/racking structures | Fixed-tilt or single-axis tracker | 70–80% imported |
| DC cabling & conduit | Panel to inverter | 40% local, 60% imported |
| AC cabling | Inverter to transformer | 50% local |
| MV/HV transformers | Step-up to grid voltage | 60–70% imported |
| SCADA & monitoring | Plant performance management | 90% imported |
| Protection & switchgear | MV/HV switchgear | 70% imported |
| Civil works (foundations, roads) | Groundworks, cable trenching | 80–90% local |
| Grid connection (up to POI) | HV line to substation | Mixed |

### 2.2 Key BOS Suppliers Active in Peru

**Inverters:**
- Huawei (dominant market share, aggressive pricing, FEOC risk consideration)
- SMA (European, premium reliability)
- Sungrow (Chinese, mid-tier)
- ABB / Hitachi Energy (MV/HV integration)

**Mounting & Trackers:**
- Nextracker (US, leading tracker supplier in Latam)
- Array Technologies (US)
- Soltec (Spanish, growing Latam presence)
- Local fabricators for fixed-tilt steel structures in Arequipa/Lima industrial zones

**Cables:**
- Conductores Eléctricos Peruanos (local production, DC cables)
- Indeco (local, Nexans group)
- Phelps Dodge / Belden (imported for specialised HV)

**Civil Contractors (BOS civil scope):**
- Cosapi (Peru's largest civil contractor)
- JJC Contratistas Generales
- Mota-Engil Peru
- Sacyr (Spanish, active in Latam)

### 2.3 Cost Benchmarks — Solar BOS
Typical BOS cost as % of total project CAPEX (utility-scale Peru):

| Component | % of Total CAPEX |
|---|---|
| PV Modules | 30–35% |
| Inverters | 7–10% |
| Mounting / Trackers | 10–14% |
| Cabling (DC+AC) | 5–7% |
| Transformers & Switchgear | 6–8% |
| Civil Works | 8–12% |
| SCADA & Monitoring | 1–2% |
| Grid Connection | 5–15% (highly variable) |
| **Total BOS (ex-modules)** | **~40–55% of CAPEX** |

*Typical all-in CAPEX: $700–900/kW for utility-scale solar in Peru (2025)*
*Grid connection highly variable — projects in Ica near existing lines ~$30–50/kW; remote sites up to $150+/kW*

## 3. BOP — Wind Projects

### 3.1 Definition & Scope
Balance of Plant (BOP) for wind encompasses all components **excluding the wind turbines**:

| Component | Description | Local/Import |
|---|---|---|
| Civil works | Roads, crane pads, foundations | 85–90% local |
| Turbine foundations | Concrete, rebar, anchor bolts | 80% local (materials), design imported |
| Internal collection grid | MV cabling, ring/radial layout | 50% local |
| Substation (onsite) | Transformer, switchgear, controls | 60–70% imported |
| Grid connection line | HV transmission to POI | Mixed |
| SCADA / communications | Wind farm management system | 90% imported (often OEM-supplied) |
| Meteorological towers | Wind measurement | Imported equipment, local install |
| O&M facility | Buildings, access roads | 100% local |

### 3.2 Wind Turbines (OEM Supply — Not BOP but Context)
Wind turbines are a critical boundary condition for BOP procurement:

| OEM | Presence in Peru | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Vestas | Active — existing projects | Danish, strong Latam service network |
| Siemens Gamesa | Active | Spanish/German, competitive on large projects |
| GE Vernova | Limited | US, supply chain via Brazil |
| Goldwind | Growing | Chinese OEM, lower capex, FEOC scrutiny risk |
| Envision | Emerging | Chinese, new entrant |

Turbine OEMs typically supply:
- Turbines (nacelle, rotor, tower)
- Foundation design specifications
- SCADA integration interface
- Commissioning supervision
BOP contractor must interface closely with OEM on foundation bolt patterns, internal grid voltage, and control system integration.

### 3.3 Key BOP Suppliers & Contractors

**Civil / Foundation Works:**
- Cosapi — dominant, track record on Cupisnique, Talara wind farms
- Graña y Montero (GMD / Stracon) — strong civil capability
- JJC Contratistas
- International: Ferrovial Construcción, FCC Construcción (Spanish)

**Electrical — Substation & Collection Grid:**
- ABB / Hitachi Energy — MV/HV transformers, switchgear
- Schneider Electric (Peru office, strong distribution network)
- Siemens Energy
- COMSA (Spanish EPC for electrical infrastructure)

**HV Grid Connection:**
- Red de Energía del Perú (REP) — transmission operator
- ISA / Interconexión Eléctrica (ISA) — Colombian, major transmission player in Peru
- Cobra Instalaciones (ACS Group)

### 3.4 Cost Benchmarks — Wind BOP
Typical BOP cost as % of total project CAPEX (onshore wind Peru):

| Component | % of Total CAPEX |
|---|---|
| Wind Turbines (supply only) | 40–50% |
| Civil Works (roads, pads, founds) | 10–15% |
| Internal Collection Grid | 5–8% |
| Substation (onsite) | 4–6% |
| Grid Connection to POI | 5–20% (highly variable) |
| SCADA & Comms | 1–2% |
| O&M Facility & Misc | 1–3% |
| **Total BOP (ex-turbines)** | **~30–45% of CAPEX** |

*Typical all-in CAPEX: $1,100–1,400/kW for onshore wind in Peru (2025)*
*Coastal/flat sites (Piura, Ica) at lower end; Andean or remote sites significantly higher*

## 4. Supply Chain Analysis

### 4.1 Import Dependency Map
Peru has no domestic manufacturing of:
- PV modules (100% imported — China dominant, ~80% market share)
- Wind turbines (100% imported)
- Inverters (100% imported)
- MV/HV transformers (>70% imported)
- Tracker systems (>70% imported)

Peru **does** have capability in:
- Civil construction (strong local contractors)
- Basic cable manufacturing (DC, AC distribution cables)
- Steel fabrication (limited, for simple structures)
- Concrete and aggregate supply
- Labour (skilled and semi-skilled, competitive cost)

### 4.2 Import Origin & FEOC Considerations
China dominates Peru's renewable equipment imports:
- ~80% of PV modules
- ~60% of inverters
- ~40-50% of structural steel components
- Growing share of wind turbine supply (Goldwind, Envision)

**FEOC Risk:** US-imposed Foreign Entity of Concern sourcing rules are relevant only for US-funded projects. For Statkraft (European developer, European financing), FEOC is not currently a regulatory constraint in Peru. However, ESG/reputational scrutiny from European lenders (IFC, EBRD-aligned standards) increasingly questions Xinjiang-linked supply chains. Worth monitoring.

### 4.3 Logistics & Lead Time Challenges
- **Port capacity:** Callao (Lima) handles most equipment imports. Oversized cargo (turbine blades, tower sections, transformers) requires specialised port handling and convoy logistics — lead times for large wind projects 12–18 months from order to site
- **Road infrastructure:** Access roads to remote wind/solar sites in Ica, Piura coastal corridors generally manageable. Andean sites (if developed) require significant civil investment
- **Customs:** Peru has FTAs with China, US, EU. Import duties on renewable energy equipment are generally low (0–6%), but customs clearance time variability is a procurement risk (30–90 day variance reported)
- **Currency:** PEN (sol) is relatively stable. Contracts typically dollar-denominated for equipment; local labour/civil in PEN — natural hedge for BOP civil scope

## 5. Regulatory & Market Environment

### 5.1 Permitting Framework
Projects require approvals from:
- **MINEM** — generation concession (final step before construction)
- **COES** — pre-operability study approval, grid connection studies
- **ANA** — water authority (relevant for concrete, dust control)
- **SENACE** — environmental impact assessment
- **Municipalities** — land use, construction permits

**Critical bottleneck:** Only 19 of 114 pre-approved projects have received MINEM final concessions. The permitting queue is the primary constraint on procurement volume activation.

### 5.2 Offtake & Bankability
- **Spot market marginal cost:** ~$25–30/MWh (spikes to $70–80/MWh in droughts)
- **No established long-term PPA market** — key structural weakness
- **Law 32249 (January 2026):** Introduces ancillary services market, distribution auction mechanisms. Critical for project bankability going forward
- Without a PPA, no project is financeable. This is the single most important non-procurement variable affecting BOS/BOP procurement timelines.

### 5.3 Local Content
Peru does not currently mandate local content percentages for renewable energy projects. However:
- Development banks (IDB, CAF) may have local employment requirements
- Community benefit agreements increasingly expected
- Procurement strategy should maximise local civil contractor participation to support social licence

## 6. Procurement Risks & Mitigations

| Risk | Severity | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Equipment supply bottleneck (inverters, transformers) | High | Medium | Long-lead procurement 12–18 months ahead; frame agreements with 2+ suppliers |
| Grid connection delay | High | High | Engage COES/REP early; build grid cost contingency 15–20% |
| Contractor capacity constraint (civil) | Medium | High | Secure Cosapi/JJC early; consider early works contracts |
| Currency/inflation on civil scope | Medium | Medium | PEN-denominated contracts with CPI adjustment clauses |
| FEOC/ESG scrutiny on Chinese equipment | Low–Medium | Low | Document supply chain due diligence; diversify where cost-competitive |
| Customs clearance delay | Medium | Medium | Buffer lead times; use customs broker with renewable experience |
| Permitting delay | High | High | Not a procurement risk per se, but gate-keep procurement spend to concession milestones |
| Single-source turbine/inverter dependency | High | Low–Medium | Dual-source strategy; avoid sole-source beyond frame agreements |

## 7. Recommendations for Statkraft Procurement

### 7.1 Near-Term Actions (Pre-FID)
1. **Establish market intelligence baseline** — map active EPC contractors, equipment suppliers, and their current workload commitments across Peru's pipeline
2. **Pre-qualify BOP civil contractors** — Cosapi, JJC, Mota-Engil at minimum; assess capacity against Statkraft project schedule
3. **Engage transformer and HV switchgear suppliers** — 18–24 month lead items; supply constraint is global, not just Peru
4. **Frame agreement discussions with tracker suppliers** — Nextracker/Array Tech have strong Latam coverage; early engagement improves pricing leverage

### 7.2 BOS/BOP Strategy Principles
- **Split packages strategically:** Separate turbine/module supply from BOP/BOS civil scope. Local BOP contracts improve community relations and reduce FX exposure
- **Drive competition:** Peru's EPC market has enough qualified contractors for competitive tension on civil scope. Don't default to single-bid
- **Grid connection: own it** — Grid connection should be owner-procured, not wrapped in EPC. Peru's transmission constraints mean third-party EPC contractors will add risk premium and have less leverage with REP/ISA
- **SCADA: OEM-integrated preferred** — For both solar and wind, SCADA systems tightly integrated with the generating equipment. Avoid attempting to separate

### 7.3 5-Year Procurement Outlook
If 30–40% of the 20 GW pipeline reaches FID by 2030:
- **6,000–8,000 MW** of new capacity requiring BOS/BOP procurement
- Estimated BOS/BOP procurement value: **$3–5 billion** across the market
- Statkraft's share (if 1–2 GW developed): **$400M–$900M** in BOS/BOP contracts

**The window to establish supplier relationships, pre-qualify contractors, and lock in capacity is now — before the market heats up.**
